At first the identification of prime numbers might seem like a slightly esoteric pursuit. We will use our knowledge of prime and composite numbers repeatedly in this course but for those concerned, I will break down some common applications.

Simplification of fractions and ratios

The most immediate use of prime numbers for most students will be the simplification of fractions and ratios. If we see a number that we know to be prime in either the numerator or denominator of a fraction, we know that the entire fraction is very likely already in simplest form. The only situation where this wouldn't be the case would be if the other value in the fraction was a multiple of our identified prime.

The same hold true for ratios. If either side of a ratio is prime, the ratio can only be simplified if the other side of the ratio is a direct multipleof the prime. By recognizing prime numbers, we can skip a lot of guessing and checking when it comes to simplification

Pattern Recognition

By understanding that every composite number is a product of primes, we can break them down into constituent parts and rearrange expressions so that they may be easier to git compute. We will see put this to more advanced use later in this course when we learn about expressions but even now it may ocassionally prove useful for mental math. For isntance, the calculating product of 15 and 7 may not be intuitive to most people but by recognizing that 15 is the product of 3 and 5, I can rewrite the expression 7x15 as 7x3x5

7x3 is instantly recognizable as 21 for most students. When multiplying any number by 5, you can use the trick of halving the number and then moving the decimal one place right. 21x5 is equivalent to (21/2)x10 or 105

Beyond this course

While it's outside the scope of this course, the fact that multiplying two prime numbers is relatively simple but factoring a composite can be tricky is central to computing systems and encryption. By taking two extremely large prime numbers and multiplying them together, I end up with a composite number made of only two factors. If I gave someone else this large composite number but didn't tell them about the primes I sued, it would be extremely difficult to compute the original primes. This time of one-way math is the basis for how most computer passwords work.
