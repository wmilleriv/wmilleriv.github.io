Calculators and Math

Parents and guardians of a similar age to myself may vividly remember being told in school, “You aren’t always going to have a calculator in your pocket.”

In hindsight, this statement seems laughable as the smartphones which have become ubiquitous in today’s world are much more than simple calculators. They have more computing power than even the beefiest of personal computers available around the turn of the last century and through the internet, have access to tools and power that our teacher’s couldn’t even imagine.

The question has now become, what benefit is there in restricting access to readily available tools? Nobody would think of forcing a lumberjack to use an axe when a chainsaw is at hand. Inventing some scenario where a tree must be felled without access to electricity or fuel is also silly. We should be preparing students for the “real world” not apocalyptic survivalism.

As a technology teacher, and the holder of a degree in computer science, I am far from a Luddite. I always encourage to think about what their tools are and which is best suited for the job at hand.

That said, the focus of my math class is to help students hone and develop all of their tools. With practice, it is possible to sharpen mental math skills to the point that even pulling a phone from your pocket and opening a calculator app is a waste of time for most arithmetic of integers and small terminating decimals.

At its core, math is about recognizing and applying patterns. In this course, students will not just learn how to calculate answers but how to examine and rearrange problems so that they may be simplified. There are almost always multiple ways to approach any given problem and this extends to something as seemingly simple as basic arithmetic.

For instance, given a problem like 7x47 a student might consider several approaches:

The most obvious approach is to use the multiplication algorithm you likely learned in school. Multiplying 7x7 to get 49, writing the 9 down and carrying the 4. Then multiplying 7x4 to get 28. Next, 4 is added to 28 to get 32 and this is written down to the left of the 9 for a final solution of 7x47=329

Alternatively, a faster solution might be rounding 47 to 50. 7x50 is relatively trivial as its just 7x5x10 or 35x10 which equals 350. Since 50 is 3 more than the original multiple of 47. I can multiply 7x3 to get 21 and subtract this from 350. Once again, the result is 7x47=329. Many people do this kind of rounding and adjusting math in their head and my not even be aware that it works because of distribution. Essentially, they are rewriting the problem 7x47 as 7(50-3).

A third (though by all means not final) solution is to apply additive distribution from left to right. This is often one of the fastest ways to do mental multiplication and doesn’t require any subtraction which is often more prone to errors for many people. Instead of calculating 7x47 directly, I calculate 7x40 to get 280 and add this to the product of 7x7. Once again we are leveraging the distributive property by imagining the problem as 7(40+7) which is equivalent to (7x40)+(7x7)

While I generally avoid the full multiplication algorithm when possible any preference for method two or three depends entirely on the given problem. It is up to the student to determine the best option for the problem in front of them.

By treating math problems as puzzles with multiple approaches, we not only sharpen our knowledge of mathematical properties but also improve the elasticity and critical thinking skills of our minds for more general use.

So after all that text, you may be wondering “but what is the policy on calculators?”

For the majority of assignments, calculator usage is allowed. I will not be dedicating time to teaching students how to use their specific device so students should familiarize themselves with any special functions they feel are useful.

There will however be approximately weekly mental math checkups during which all electronic device use will be restricted. If your child has an IEP for calculator usage, I encourage you to read about my specific policies on IEP implementation and contact me or the special educators for further questions or concerns.

I will also encourage and potentially enforce restrictions on students I see overly relying on calculators or other electronic tools.
